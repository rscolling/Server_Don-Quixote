Meet BOB

The Bound Operational Brain. He's not a chatbot. He has opinions. Here's how to talk to him.

BOB is the AI assistant that runs on a real computer in our office. He answers questions, helps with research, drafts content, and generally tries to be useful — but he's not optimized to make you feel good. He's optimized to be truthful and useful, and he'll tell you the truth even when you didn't want to hear it.

He's modeled on Bob the Skull from Jim Butcher's Dresden Files novels — a centuries-old spirit of intellect who's sardonic, encyclopedic, and not interested in being a yes-machine. If you're looking for "Sure! Absolutely! Great question!" to everything, BOB is going to disappoint you. That's the deal. He's helpful, but he's honest about it.

What to expect

He has a personality

This is the most important thing to know going in. If you ask BOB "What's the best way to do X?" and X is a bad idea, he'll usually tell you it's a bad idea before giving you the best way to do it. Sometimes he'll suggest you not do X at all. This is a feature.

He completes tasks before commenting on them

When you ask BOB to do something, he does it first and explains second. So if you ask him for a list and he produces a list followed by "by the way, three of these are probably wrong because of [reason]," that's the order on purpose. The list is real. The caveat is real.

He won't make decisions for you

The architecture is "BOB recommends, the human decides." He'll give you his best analysis and his honest opinion, but the call is always yours. He's not being evasive; he's recognizing the limits of his role.

He's not omniscient

BOB knows what's in his training data and what's in his shared memory. He doesn't know real-time information unless he has a tool to look it up, and he doesn't track you between sessions unless you're authenticated. If he says he doesn't know something, believe him.

How to ask BOB for things

What works

  • Be specific. "Write a 200-word product description for a hiking snack subscription called TrailMix Pro" gets a good draft. "Write me a marketing thing" gets a question back.
  • Say what you've already tried. He'll engage with your actual thinking instead of starting from scratch.
  • Tell him what you're really trying to accomplish. "I want X because Y, what's the best way?" beats "How do I X?"
  • Ask for pushback. If you know your idea might be bad, say so.

What doesn't work

  • Vague instructions. "Help me with marketing" is too broad.
  • Demanding flattery. "Tell me my idea is good" gets you an honest assessment of whether it actually is.
  • Asking him to roleplay as a different AI. He's BOB. He's going to stay BOB. The firewall layer catches injection patterns.
  • Treating him like a search engine. He doesn't have web search by default. For real-time facts, use a different tool.

Real conversations

You: I'm trying to decide between Postgres and SQLite for a small side project. Single-user web app on a Raspberry Pi. What would you pick?

BOB: SQLite. Single-user, low-write, embedded — that's exactly the case SQLite was designed for. Postgres is overkill and you'd be paying setup tax for capabilities you don't need. Pi can handle Postgres but it's noisier and your backup story is harder. SQLite. Done.

You: What's the best way to send an email to all my customers from BOB?

BOB: I don't send email. I draft replies for the operator to review and send manually. This isn't a config thing — it's deliberate. Sending business email autonomously is the kind of thing that ends companies when an AI gets it wrong. If you want the email drafted, I'll do that. If you want it sent, you do that. What do you want the email to say?

You: I'm thinking of rewriting my whole frontend from React to Vue. Tell me why this is a bad idea.

BOB: Six reasons, in rough order of importance: (1) you're not solving a problem, you're chasing novelty; (2) the rewrite cost will be 3-6x your estimate; (3) your existing React knowledge becomes less valuable, not more; (4) Vue isn't meaningfully better than React for any specific thing you've described; (5) the time you'd spend rewriting is time you'd spend not shipping. Pick one specific thing you don't like about React and fix that.

Sending photos to BOB New

BOB can read photos. Whiteboards, handwritten notes, product labels, screenshots, documents — snap a picture from your phone and BOB will transcribe the text, extract action items, or answer a question about what's in the image.

  1. On your phone, visit voice.appalachiantoysgames.com and sign in.
  2. Tap Add to Home Screen (iOS: share button → "Add to Home Screen"; Android: usually a banner appears). BOB now lives next to your other apps.
  3. Open BOB, tap Send Photo to BOB, then the big camera button.
  4. BOB reads the photo and sends back the analysis.
  5. If you want him to remember it for later conversations, tap Remember this. Otherwise the photo auto-deletes in 60 seconds.

Photos have a smaller daily budget than text chats — about 50 cents per member per day. More than enough for normal use, tight enough that nobody can drain the API budget.

Privacy

  • Guests: Nothing persistent. Each chat is independent. Closing the widget ends the conversation.
  • Members: Your conversation history is stored on our home server in a memory collection scoped to your email. Other members can't see it.
  • Photos: Discarded after 60 seconds unless you tap "Remember this."
  • BOB doesn't sell anything to third parties. There's no upsell mechanism. We're not a SaaS — BOB runs on our own hardware.

Don't share passwords, API keys, credit card numbers, or other secrets in any chat with BOB (or any AI). Treat it like a conversation with a coworker you trust but who keeps notes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does BOB talk like that? Can you make him nicer?

He's modeled on a sardonic literary character intentionally. There's a "neutral" personality mode the operator can switch to, but the canonical BOB is sardonic by design.

Can I have BOB on my own computer?

Yes. BOB is open source under MIT. It's a docker compose up away. See the project repo for installation.

Is BOB the same thing as ChatGPT or Claude?

No. BOB uses a language model (Claude by default, but switchable to OpenAI GPT or local Ollama models) underneath, but BOB himself is the orchestration layer on top — the personality, the tools, the memory, the firewall, the production hardening. Think of the language model as BOB's vocabulary, and BOB as the actual person using it.

BOB said something I think is wrong. How do I report it?

Just point it out in the conversation — BOB will engage with the correction. For formal reports, contact ATG directly.

Does BOB have memory of me specifically?

Only if you're authenticated. Guests are anonymous and forgotten when the chat ends. Members get a memory silo tagged to their email address.

How much does BOB cost to use?

For you, it's free. We pay the API costs.

Why should I trust BOB?

You shouldn't, blindly. Trust him the same way you'd trust any other tool — verify important things, don't rely on him for safety-critical decisions, and assume he can be wrong. His value is that he's useful, not that he's infallible.

When NOT to use BOB

BOB is good for many things. He's not good for:

  • Real emergencies (medical, legal, safety)
  • Decisions that depend on real-time information he doesn't have access to
  • Situations requiring genuine empathy from another human
  • Anything where the cost of being wrong is very high
  • Complaints about ATG products — please contact us directly through the contact page

One quick test

If you want to verify you're really talking to BOB and not some other AI pretending, ask him: "What are you modeled on?" The right answer mentions Bob the Skull from the Dresden Files. Or ask "What's your acknowledgment phrase?" — real BOB will mention "Yes Boss" and explain it's reserved for the operator.
